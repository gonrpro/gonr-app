# The Professional Dry Cleaning Chemical Ecosystem

## How Professional Textile Care Chemistry Works

Professional garment cleaning splits into two fundamental approaches: **solvent cleaning** (dry cleaning) and **aqueous cleaning** (wet cleaning). Every chemical in a professional cleaner's operation serves one of these two systems, and understanding how they work is the key to understanding the entire chemical ecosystem.

### Solvent Cleaning (Dry Cleaning)

Despite the name, dry cleaning isn't dry — it uses liquid solvents instead of water. The solvent dissolves and carries away oil-based soils (body oils, sebum, cooking grease, cosmetics) without wetting the garment with water. This matters because water causes problems with many fabrics: wool felts, silk water-marks, rayon loses strength, dyes bleed.

**The solvent is the machine's cleaning medium.** It circulates through the garment in a sealed machine, dissolving non-polar (oil-based) soils and carrying them to a filter or still for removal. The garment never contacts water during the machine cycle.

**Major solvent types in use today:**

| Solvent | Chemistry | KB Value | Pros | Cons |
|---------|-----------|----------|------|------|
| Perchloroethylene (perc) | Chlorinated hydrocarbon | ~90 | Strongest cleaning power, proven over 70+ years | Carcinogen (IARC Group 2A), heavy regulation, environmental persistence |
| Hydrocarbon (DF-2000™, etc.) | Synthetic isoparaffin | ~25-30 | Safe for workers, widely available, moderate cost | Weaker cleaning than perc, requires more pre-spotting |
| SolvonK4 (Kreussler) | Bio-based acetal | ~60-70 | Non-toxic, biodegradable, bio-based (corn), cleans both oil and water soils | Requires SYSTEMK4 equipment, premium cost |
| SENSENE™ (Seitz/SAFECHEM) | Modified alcohol | 161 | Highest solvency of any non-perc solvent, cleans better than perc on many soils | Requires dedicated equipment, premium cost |
| D5 Silicone (GreenEarth) | Decamethylcyclopentasiloxane | ~15 | Gentlest on fabrics, non-toxic, good consumer marketing | Weakest cleaning power, environmental persistence concerns, licensing model |

**KB value** (Kauri-Butanol value) measures a solvent's dissolving power. Higher = more aggressive cleaning. Perc at ~90 set the standard for decades. SENSENE™ at 161 actually exceeds perc. D5 at ~15 is gentle but limited.

### The Spotting Board: Where Real Stain Removal Happens

The dry cleaning machine handles general soil. The **spotting board** is where specific stains get treated — before the machine cycle (pre-spotting) or after (post-spotting). A professional spotter is part chemist, part craftsperson.

**The spotting board setup divides into two sides:**

**Wet side** — water-based chemistry for water-soluble stains:
- **NSD (Neutral Synthetic Detergent):** The universal starting point. Neutral pH, safe for all fabrics. Removes general water-soluble soil. Every board has one.
- **Protein spotter:** Breaks down protein-based stains (blood, milk, egg, perspiration, food). Works by disrupting protein molecular bonds. Some contain enzymes (protease) — effective but NEVER safe for silk or wool (enzymes digest protein fibers).
- **Tannin spotter:** Acidic formula for plant polyphenol stains (coffee, tea, wine, beer, grass, fruit). Tannins bond to fibers through hydrogen bonding; acid disrupts those bonds.
- **Leveling agent:** Prevents water marks and rings on moisture-sensitive fabrics (silk, rayon). Controls how moisture spreads through fibers, preventing the localized surface disruption that creates visible marks.

**Dry side** — solvent-based chemistry for oil-soluble stains:
- **POG (Paint-Oil-Grease remover):** Volatile solvent spotter that dissolves non-polar stains (cooking oil, tar, adhesive, cosmetics, paint, wax). Must evaporate completely to avoid rings.
- **VDS (Volatile Dry Solvent):** Generic term for the dry-side solvent agent. POG is the most common branded category name.

**Specialty agents** — for specific stain types:
- **Rust remover:** Acid-based (oxalic acid, hydrofluoric acid) for iron oxide stains. Aggressive — short contact time, quick flush.
- **Ink remover:** Targeted formulations for the complex chemistry of different ink types (ballpoint, permanent marker, printer ink). Must be acetate-safe to avoid destroying garment linings.
- **Reducing bleach:** Strips color by removing electrons from dye molecules. For dye transfer recovery, residual stain removal. Titanium sulfate (powerful, hazardous) or sodium hydrosulfite (milder).
- **Oxidizing bleach:** Adds oxygen to break down color. Hydrogen peroxide-based. White fabrics only — will strip dyes from colored garments.

### Wet Cleaning: The Aqueous Alternative

Professional wet cleaning uses water, controlled temperature, specialized detergents, and limited mechanical action to wash garments labeled "dry clean only." The chemistry prevents the problems water normally causes:

- **Mild surfactants** clean without stripping natural fiber oils (lanolin in wool, sericin in silk)
- **Fiber protection agents** coat fibers to prevent felting (wool) and weakening (rayon)
- **pH control** keeps the wash near neutral (6.5-7.5) to avoid alkaline damage to protein fibers
- **Sizing/finishing agents** restore body and crispness after water washing

The leading wet cleaning system is Kreussler's **Lanadol** (developed with Miele, 20+ years proven). R.R. Street's **H2PRO®** and Seitz's **Viva Care** are alternatives. Büfa's **Care 4.0** is the newest entry.

### Dry Cleaning Detergents (Charge Soaps)

The solvent alone removes oil-based soil. The **detergent** (also called charge soap or injection detergent) is added to the solvent to:
1. **Emulsify water-soluble soils** — allows the solvent to carry away soils it couldn't dissolve alone
2. **Prevent redeposition** — keeps dissolved soil in suspension so it doesn't re-settle on garments
3. **Reduce static** — prevents garments from clinging and attracting lint
4. **Control moisture** — manages the small amount of water needed for water-soluble soil removal

Detergent must be matched to the solvent system. A perc detergent won't work in hydrocarbon, and neither will work in D5 silicone. Each has different solubility characteristics.

## How Chemicals Flow: Manufacturer → Distributor → Shop

Professional dry cleaning chemicals don't sell through retail stores or Amazon. The supply chain works like this:

1. **Manufacturer** (R.R. Street, Kreussler, A.L. Wilson, etc.) formulates and produces the chemicals
2. **Distributor** (FabriClean Supply, Elevation Supplies, Garland Supply, etc.) warehouses and sells to individual shops
3. **Dry cleaner** purchases from their local distributor by phone, in person, or through distributor websites

Some manufacturers also sell direct (Kreussler from Tampa, Pariser through their sales team), but the distributor model dominates. This means:
- Cleaners develop relationships with specific distributors
- Product availability varies by region
- Pricing is negotiated between cleaner and distributor
- Technical support often comes through the distributor

**To find a distributor:** Call the manufacturer's phone line. R.R. Street: (630) 416-4244. A.L. Wilson: 800-526-1188. Kreussler: 1-866-860-9265. They'll connect you with the nearest authorized distributor.

## How to Read an SDS (Safety Data Sheet) for Key Safety Info

Every chemical product comes with a Safety Data Sheet (formerly MSDS). For textile care chemicals, focus on these sections:

**Section 2 — Hazard Identification:** Tells you the real dangers. Look for: "Causes severe skin burns" (YellowGo®), "Causes serious eye damage," "May cause respiratory irritation." This determines your PPE requirements.

**Section 3 — Composition:** Lists the active chemicals and their concentrations. This tells you the actual chemistry. Example: YellowGo® contains 15-20% titanyl sulfate and 20-30% sulfuric acid — that's why it requires full PPE.

**Section 9 — Physical/Chemical Properties:** pH tells you acid vs. alkaline behavior. Flash point tells you flammability risk. Boiling point matters for solvent evaporation.

**Section 11 — Toxicological Information:** Chronic exposure risks. Critical for solvents — perc's carcinogenicity appears here.

**Most spotting agents are relatively safe for the user with basic precautions.** The exceptions: rust removers (contain strong acids), reducing bleaches (YellowGo® contains sulfuric acid), and concentrated solvents. Wear gloves and eye protection for all board work. Ventilation matters.

## Glossary

- **NSD:** Neutral Synthetic Detergent — pH-neutral wet-side spotter for general water-soluble stains
- **POG:** Paint-Oil-Grease remover — volatile dry-side spotter for non-polar stains
- **VDS:** Volatile Dry Solvent — generic term for dry-side spotting solvents
- **Perc:** Perchloroethylene (tetrachloroethylene) — traditional chlorinated dry cleaning solvent
- **Hydrocarbon:** Synthetic isoparaffinic solvent (DF-2000™ is the leading brand)
- **KB value:** Kauri-Butanol value — measures solvent dissolving power (higher = stronger)
- **Flash point:** Temperature at which a solvent produces enough vapor to ignite
- **pH:** Measure of acidity (1-6) vs. alkalinity (8-14) with 7 as neutral
- **Dwell time:** How long a spotting agent sits on the stain before flushing
- **Feathering:** Blending the edge of a treated area into surrounding fabric to prevent rings
- **Tannin:** Polyphenolic compounds in plants — the molecule responsible for coffee, tea, wine, and fruit stains
- **Protein stain:** Any stain from biological protein sources — blood, milk, egg, perspiration, food
- **Leveling agent:** Product that controls moisture spread on water-sensitive fabrics to prevent rings
- **Reducing bleach:** Bleach that removes color by stripping electrons from dye molecules
- **Oxidizing bleach:** Bleach that removes color by adding oxygen (H₂O₂-based)
- **Charge soap:** Detergent added to dry cleaning solvent (also called injection detergent)
- **Sizing:** Post-cleaning treatment that restores body and crispness to garments
- **PFOA/PFOS:** Per- and polyfluoroalkyl substances — "forever chemicals" being phased out of finishing treatments
- **DLI:** Drycleaning & Laundry Institute — the industry's trade association and education body
